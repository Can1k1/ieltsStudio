Many graduates struggle to find work, and some commentators argue that universities should emphasise practical training rather than abstract theory. I largely agree, although academic knowledge still has a place in certain professions.

On one hand, theoretical courses develop critical thinking. For example, law and medicine require deep conceptual understanding before students practise in hospitals or courts. Employers in these fields expect graduates to have mastered core principles, not only short vocational modules.

On the other hand, numerous graduates in business or information technology leave university without workplace-ready skills. A 2022 survey in Germany reported that nearly thirty percent of recent graduates felt unprepared for their first role. When programmes include internships, placements, or project-based assessments, employment outcomes improve.

In conclusion, universities should integrate practical components into most degrees while preserving rigorous theory where it is essential. This balanced approach would better serve both students and employers.
