Graduate unemployment has prompted debate about whether universities should prioritise vocational training over theoretical study. I strongly agree that practical skills should receive greater emphasis, though I do not believe theory should be discarded entirely.

Advocates of theory argue that abstract learning cultivates analytical ability. In disciplines such as engineering or economics, students must understand models before they can innovate. Without this foundation, graduates may apply techniques mechanically without evaluating their limitations.

Nevertheless, in many saturated labour markets, employers increasingly demand demonstrable competencies. For instance, software firms often require portfolios of completed applications rather than transcripts alone. Programmes that embed industry certifications, supervised internships, and collaborative projects enable students to transition smoothly into employment. In Finland, polytechnic reforms in 2018 reportedly reduced graduate unemployment among technology students within two years.

Moreover, focusing exclusively on theory can misalign curricula with economic realities. When graduates accumulate debt yet remain underemployed, public confidence in higher education declines. Governments therefore have an interest in funding partnerships between universities and local enterprises.

To conclude, although theoretical study remains indispensable in selected fields, universities should restructure most programmes so that practical competence is assessed alongside academic knowledge. Such reform would address youth unemployment without reducing educational standards.
